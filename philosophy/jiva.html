Five schools of Indian philosophy, compared

What each school says

Advaita Vedanta
In Advaita philosophy, the concept of jiva is closely related to atman. According to some sources, atman is identical to jiva, referring to "the individual soul" (cite: edge_018767) or "the embodied soul" (cite: edge_020627). However, other sources suggest that atman is distinct from jiva, with "a fancied difference between the individual soul and the intelligent Self" (cite: edge_020676). Additionally, atman is also described as a qualified aspect of jiva, being "a particular part of that soul that is circumscribed by that body" (cite: edge_017959).
Dvaita Vedanta
In the school of Dvaita, the concept of jiva is closely related to atman, with some sources suggesting they are identical (cite: edge_007307). The jiva is also seen as eligible for performing sacrifices, which is a qualified aspect of dharma (cite: edge_007781). Additionally, the jiva is considered the cause of karma (cite: edge_008066) and is distinct from moksha (cite: edge_008065). The jiva is also associated with the mind, as the mind is considered identical to the jiva (cite: edge_007376).
Vishishtadvaita
In the school of Vishishtadvaita, the concept of jiva is closely related to Brahman, with Brahman being the cause of jiva (cite: edge_006235). However, Brahman is also distinct from jiva (cite: edge_005431). The jiva is considered to be identical to the atman, or the individual self (cite: edge_002055, edge_005491, edge_005992). This identity between jiva and atman is a central idea in Vishishtadvaita, with the jiva being seen as an aspect of the atman (cite: edge_000148) and the atman being the essence of the jiva (cite: edge_005993). Achintya Bhedabheda
In the achintya_bhedabheda school of philosophy, the concept of jiva is complex. The jiva is distinct from Krsna, the Supreme Personality of Godhead, as it envies Him (cite: edge_003455). However, the jiva is also considered a qualified aspect of Krsna (cite: edge_003225) and the Supreme Lord (cite: edge_003207). The jiva is entangled in the cycle of karma, with its actions causing karma (cite: edge_004160) and being affected by it (cite: edge_002842).
Dvaitadvaita
According to the dvaitadvaita school of philosophy, the concept of jiva is closely related to the atman. In general, it is stated that the atman is identical to the jiva (cite: edge_027194). This idea is supported by various sources, including the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, which describes the atman as "thy Self, the puller (ruler) within, the immortal" (cite: edge_026930). The atman is also seen as the cause of the jiva, with the Maitri Upanishad stating that "by him has this body been made intelligent" (cite: edge_028016). Additionally, the atman is considered a qualified aspect of the jiva, as described in the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad as "mounted by the intelligent Self" (cite: edge_027197).
Buddhism (Pali Canon)
In Buddhist philosophy, the concept of jiva is closely related to the body, as it is described as being "tamed, bearing their final body" (cite: edge_009912). Jiva is also seen as a cause of samsara, which is linked to a state of "sleep" (cite: edge_010086). Additionally, jiva is considered distinct from the idea of prolonged life, highlighting that they are not comparable (cite: edge_009470). This distinction suggests that jiva is a unique concept within Buddhist thought, separate from notions of extended lifespan (cite: edge_009470).
