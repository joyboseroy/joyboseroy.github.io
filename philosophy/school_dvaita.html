Atman
In the school of Dvaita, the atman is seen as the cause of karma, as stated in various sources, including "the body which is produced by the Kar- ma" (cite: edge_007736) and "he does of necessity works each of which would be the cause of at least ten births" (cite: edge_007447). The atman is also considered a qualified aspect of dharma, with evidence such as "Indeed He only causes the soul to do the righteous deed" (cite: edge_007630) and "merit accruing to the priests also" (cite: edge_007869). Additionally, the atman is linked to karma through phrases like "activity and release result" (cite: edge_007351) and "passing by force of Karma" (cite: edge_007201). The atman's relationship with karma and dharma is a central theme in Dvaita philosophy (cite: edge_007339).
Brahman

However, other texts imply that the atman can become one with Brahman, or is a qualified aspect of it (cite: edge_007787). The relationship between atman and Brahman is also described as causal, with the atman being able to reach or enter into Brahman (cite: edge_008028). Ultimately, the Dvaita school seems to suggest that the atman and Brahman are related but distinct entities, with the atman being able to enjoy or experience Brahman, but not being identical to it (cite: edge_008036).
Karma
In the school of Dvaita, the concept of karma is closely related to the atman, or individual self, which is considered the cause of karma (cite: edge_008060). The atman's actions and decisions lead to the accumulation of karma, which can result in various outcomes (cite: edge_007736). Dharma, or righteous living, is also seen as a qualified aspect of karma, with certain actions and duties being considered part of one's karma (cite: edge_000250). The Dvaita school emphasizes that the atman's actions have consequences, and that dharma plays a role in shaping one's karma (cite: edge_007447).
Dharma
In the school of Dvaita, dharma is seen as a qualified aspect of karma, as it is related to the paths to the lordship of the objects of enjoyment (cite: edge_000250). Dharma is also considered a qualified aspect of brahma, with reference to the different results to which the gods are entitled (cite: edge_007650). Additionally, dharma is distinct from karma, as it involves doing what is permitted, rather than acting solely on one's own accord (cite: edge_007791). Dharma is also considered a cause of moksha, as even a small act of righteousness can become great and everlasting (cite: edge_007917).

Samsara
