What each school says

Advaita Vedanta
In the Advaita school of philosophy, dharma is often associated with karma, as seen in the idea that "only the duties pertaining to the stages of life, they are also helpful in the rise of knowledge" (cite: edge_018604). Dharma is also considered to be related to the performance of virtuous deeds, which "do not come into conflict with the knowledge arising from the scriptures" (cite: edge_018708). Additionally, dharma is linked to the concept of atman, with "faith" being considered a key aspect of it (cite: edge_019772). The relationship between dharma and karma is complex, with some texts suggesting that dharma is a qualified aspect of karma, as in "enjoyment of the fruits of our deeds" (cite: edge_018991).
Dvaita Vedanta
In the school of Dvaita, dharma is seen as a qualified aspect of karma, with evidence suggesting it is related to "paths to the lordship of the objects of enjoyment" (cite: edge_000250). Dharma is also considered a qualified aspect of brahma, with reference to "the other qualities with reference to the different results to which the gods, etc., are entitled" (cite: edge_007650). However, dharma is distinct from karma, as indicated by the idea that "nothing unrighteous is to be suspected" (cite: edge_007808). Additionally, dharma is considered a cause of moksha, with the idea that "Even a small act of righteousness performed by him who has seen Brahman becomes great and everlasting" (cite: edge_007917).
Vishishtadvaita
In the Vishishtadvaita school of philosophy, dharma is closely related to ahimsa, or non-harming, as it is said "let him not harm any creature" (cite: edge_006198). Dharma is also considered an aspect of vedanta, as it is "consistent with Dharma" (cite: edge_002616). Additionally, dharma is seen as a path to moksha, or liberation, as it is said that one "becomes righteous and obtains everlasting peace" through dharma (cite: edge_001307). Dharma is also connected to karma, or action, as it is said that "success is quickly attained by men through action" (cite: edge_000559) (cite: edge_006522).

Dharma is also seen as a cause of atman, with the steady effort to hold certain thoughts awakening dormant powers (cite: edge_011726). Additionally, dharma is associated with the eternal and self-controlled aspects of atman (cite: edge_021581).
