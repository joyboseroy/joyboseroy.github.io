Advaita Vedanta
In the Advaita school of philosophy, the concept of Brahman is closely tied to the idea of Atman, with the two being considered identical (cite: edge_021429). This idea is repeated throughout various Upanishads, with statements such as "the Self is verily Brahman" (cite: edge_021430). The school also suggests that Dharma is a qualified aspect of Brahman (cite: edge_020753). Ultimately, the Advaita school emphasizes the unity of Atman and Brahman, with the Self being considered the same as Brahman (cite: edge_021404).
Dvaita Vedanta
In the Dvaita school of philosophy, the concept of Brahman is complex, with some texts suggesting that the atman (individual self) is distinct from Brahman (cite: edge_007811). However, other texts imply that the atman can become one with Brahman, or is a qualified aspect of it (cite: edge_007787). The relationship between atman and Brahman is also described as causal, with the atman being able to reach or enter into Brahman (cite: edge_008028). Ultimately, the Dvaita school seems to suggest that the atman and Brahman are related but distinct entities, with the atman being able to comprehend or experience Brahman in some way (cite: edge_007792).
Vishishtadvaita
In the Vishishtadvaita school of philosophy, the concept of Brahman is closely related to the atman, or individual soul. According to this school, the atman is a qualified aspect of Brahman, meaning it is connected to and derives from the ultimate reality of Brahman (cite: edge_006693). The atman is described as reaching Brahman through various paths, ultimately attaining true knowledge and freedom from evil (cite: edge_006683). The Vishishtadvaita school also suggests that the atman, while distinct from Brahman, is ultimately a part of it, with the highest Self being the causal substance of all (cite: edge_006703, cite: edge_006640).
