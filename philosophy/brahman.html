What each school says

Advaita Vedanta
In the school of Advaita, Brahman is considered to be identical to the atman, or the Self (cite: edge_021429). This idea is repeated throughout various Upanishads, with statements such as "the Self is verily Brahman" (cite: edge_021430). The school of Advaita also suggests that dharma, or duty, is a qualified aspect of Brahman (cite: edge_020753). Ultimately, the goal of Advaita is to realize that the atman and Brahman are one and the same (cite: edge_021404).
Dvaita Vedanta
In the Dvaita school of philosophy, the concept of Brahman is complex, with some texts suggesting that the atman (individual self) is distinct from Brahman (cite: edge_007811). However, other texts imply that the atman can become one with Brahman, or is a qualified aspect of it (cite: edge_007787). The relationship between atman and Brahman is also described as causal, with the atman being able to reach or enter into Brahman (cite: edge_008016). Ultimately, the Dvaita school seems to suggest that the atman and Brahman are related but distinct entities, with the atman being able to comprehend or experience Brahman in some way (cite: edge_007792).
Vishishtadvaita
In the Vishishtadvaita school of philosophy, the concept of Brahman is closely related to the atman, or individual soul. According to this school, the atman is a qualified aspect of Brahman, meaning it is connected to and derives from the ultimate reality of Brahman (cite: edge_006693). The atman is described as reaching Brahman through various paths, ultimately attaining true knowledge and freedom from evil (cite: edge_006683). The Vishishtadvaita school also suggests that the atman, while distinct from Brahman, is an integral part of it, with the highest Self being the causal substance of all (cite: edge_006703, cite: edge_006640). The atman's relationship with Brahman is one of qualified non-duality, with the atman possessing attributes of the individual soul while being part of the larger Brahman (cite: edge_006761). Achintya Bhedabheda
In the achintya_bhedabheda school of philosophy, the concept of brahman is closely related to the atman, or individual self. According to this school, the atman is sometimes considered identical to brahman, as stated in the Bhagavad Gita, "the Self is verily Brahman" (cite: edge_003283). At other times, the atman is seen as a qualified aspect of brahman, with the Bhagavad Gita stating that "all living beings are but part of Me" (cite: edge_003142). This school also suggests that the atman can become one with brahman, as described in the Bhagavad Gita, "becoming one with the Absolute" (cite: edge_004489). Overall, the achintya_bhedabheda school presents a complex and multifaceted understanding of the relationship between the atman and brahman (cite: edge_003142).
Dvaitadvaita
According to the Dvaitadvaita school of philosophy, the atman is identical to Brahman (cite: edge_001077). This idea is repeated consistently throughout the school's teachings, emphasizing the oneness of the individual self and the ultimate reality (cite: edge_001074). The Dvaitadvaita school suggests that one should realize the atman as one with what governs the beings, which is Brahman (cite: edge_001076). This understanding of the atman and Brahman is central to the Dvaitadvaita philosophy (cite: edge_001075).
