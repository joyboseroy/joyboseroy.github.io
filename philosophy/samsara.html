What each school says

Advaita Vedanta
In Advaita philosophy, samsara, or the cycle of birth and death, is caused by several factors, including kama, or worldly desire (cite: edge_019654), avidya, or ignorance (cite: edge_019652), and maya, or illusion (cite: edge_019887). The atman, or individual self, is also seen as a qualified aspect of samsara (cite: edge_017810), and is said to be affected by the cycle of birth and death (cite: edge_019284). Additionally, the jiva, or individual soul, is considered a qualified aspect of samsara (cite: edge_018759), highlighting the complex and multifaceted nature of this concept in Advaita thought (cite: edge_019287).
Dvaita Vedanta
In the school of Dvaita, samsara, or the cycle of birth and death, is caused by the atman (cite: edge_007003). Samsara is distinct from moksha, which is liberation from this cycle (cite: edge_008062). According to Dvaita, samsara is also the cause of creation (cite: edge_007064) and can ultimately lead to moksha by ending mistaken notions (cite: edge_007548). Additionally, samsara is obstructed by samana, and parama is the cause of samsara (cite: edge_007946, edge_007942). [Note: this summary may contain an unverified citation.]
Vishishtadvaita
In the school of Vishishtadvaita, samsara is distinct from brahman (cite: edge_000751) and moksha (cite: edge_005537). According to this school, karma is the cause of samsara (cite: edge_002203), and the atman is a qualified aspect of samsara (cite: edge_002199). This means that the atman is involved in the cycle of births and deaths that characterizes samsara (cite: edge_002199). Overall, the school of Vishishtadvaita views samsara as a state that can be overcome (cite: edge_000751).
Achintya Bhedabheda
In the achintya_bhedabheda school of philosophy, the concept of samsara is associated with the atman, which is considered the cause of samsara (cite: edge_004235). The school also views Krsna as a qualified aspect of samsara (cite: edge_003831). Generally, the atman is seen as a qualified aspect of samsara, tied to the cycle of birth and death (cite: edge_002200). Additionally, karma is considered a cause of samsara, contributing to the cycle of existence (cite: edge_021862).
Dvaitadvaita

Buddhism (Pali Canon)
In Buddhist philosophy, samsara is a concept that is distinct from the idea of atman, or self (cite: edge_008880). According to Buddhist teachings, a realized being no longer exists after death, and is neither still existing nor non-existing (cite: edge_008848). Karma, or actions, is seen as a cause of samsara (cite: edge_010040). Additionally, the actions of beings can lead to rebirth in samsara, with some beings going to a bad place (cite: edge_010181).
Jainism (general)
In Jain philosophy, the concept of samsara is closely related to the idea of karma, which is seen as a cause of samsara (cite: edge_021268). Karma is believed to lead to the cycle of birth and death, with the atman, or soul, being a qualified aspect of samsara (cite: edge_001263). The atman is also distinct from samsara, with some sources suggesting that the multeity of the soul is proved by the distribution of bondage and emancipation (cite: edge_011607). Ultimately, karma is a key factor in determining an individual's experience of samsara, with the goal of eliminating acquired karma to escape the cycle (cite: edge_021862).
