Five schools of Indian philosophy, compared

What each school says

Advaita Vedanta
In Advaita philosophy, samsara, or the cycle of birth and death, is caused by various factors including kama, avidya, and maya (cite: edge_019654, edge_019652, edge_019887). The atman, or individual self, is also seen as a qualified aspect of samsara, meaning it is connected to this cycle (cite: edge_017810, edge_019889). Additionally, the jiva, or individual soul, is considered a qualified aspect of samsara, involving birth and death (cite: edge_018759, edge_019287). Overall, samsara is viewed as a state that depends on external factors and is characterized by worldly existence and suffering (cite: edge_019887). Dvaita Vedanta
In the school of Dvaita, samsara is seen as a state caused by the atman (edge_007003). Samsara is distinct from moksha, or liberation, which it can also ultimately lead to (edge_008062, edge_007548). According to Dvaita, samsara is also the cause of creation (edge_007064) and can obstruct the attainment of certain states, such as samana (edge_007946). Additionally, parama, or the supreme reality, is considered the cause of samsara (edge_007942).
Vishishtadvaita
In the school of Vishishtadvaita, samsara is distinct from brahman (cite: edge_000751) and moksha (cite: edge_005537). Karma is considered the cause of samsara (cite: edge_002203). The atman is also seen as a qualified aspect of samsara (cite: edge_002199). This perspective suggests that samsara is a cycle that can be overcome, with the ultimate goal of achieving liberation (cite: edge_000751).
Achintya Bhedabheda
In the achintya_bhedabheda school of philosophy, the concept of samsara is associated with the atman, which is considered the cause of samsara (cite: edge_004235). The school also views Krsna as a qualified aspect of samsara (cite: edge_003831). According to this school, the atman is connected to the cycle of birth and death, which is a key aspect of samsara (cite: edge_004235). Overall, the achintya_bhedabheda school sees a complex relationship between the atman, Krsna, and samsara (cite: edge_003831).
Dvaitadvaita
According to the dvaitadvaita school of philosophy, the concept of samsara is related to the atman, with the atman being a qualified aspect of samsara (cite: edge_001263). The atman is also distinct from samsara (cite: edge_010754). Karma is considered a cause of samsara (cite: edge_021268). Additionally, dharma is also a qualified aspect of samsara (cite: edge_026119).
Buddhism (Pali Canon)
In Buddhist philosophy, samsara is a concept that is distinct from the idea of atman, or self (cite: edge_008894). According to Buddhist teachings, karma is a cause of samsara, meaning that an individual's actions can lead to their continued existence in this cycle (cite: edge_010040). The concept of atman is often described as being neither still existing nor no longer existing after death, highlighting its distinction from samsara (cite: edge_008906). Additionally, the actions of beings can also contribute to samsara, with some beings going to a bad place as a result of their actions (cite: edge_010181).
Jainism (general)
In Jain common philosophy, samsara is related to the concept of atman, with some sources suggesting that atman is a qualified aspect of samsara (cite: edge_001263). However, it's also stated that atman is distinct from samsara (cite: edge_010754). Karma is seen as a cause of samsara, according to various sources (cite: edge_021268, edge_021850, edge_021862). Additionally, dharma is also connected to samsara, with some sources indicating it's a qualified aspect of samsara (cite: edge_026119), while others suggest it's distinct from samsara (cite: edge_001191) (cite: edge_001263). Jainism (Digambara)

Additionally, karma is a key factor in the cycle of samsara, with the goal of eliminating acquired karma to escape the cycle (cite: edge_021862).
Samkhya
In the school of Samkhya, samsara is related to the concept of atman, with some sources suggesting that atman is a qualified aspect of samsara (cite: edge_001263). However, it's also stated that atman is distinct from samsara (cite: edge_010754). Karma is seen as a cause of samsara, leading to the cycle of birth and death (cite: edge_021268). The relationship between atman and samsara is complex, with atman being involved in the cycle of samsara, but also having the potential to be liberated from it (cite: edge_011607).
Yoga
In the school of yoga, the concept of samsara is related to the idea of a cycle of birth and death (cite: edge_002200). Karma, or actions, is seen as a cause of samsara, leading to a continuous cycle of existence (cite: edge_021850). The atman, or soul, is also considered to be a part of samsara, experiencing the cycle of birth and death (cite: edge_022793). Additionally, dharma, or duty, is connected to samsara, with some sources suggesting it is a distinct aspect of the cycle (cite: edge_001191).
Vaisheshika
According to the Vaisheshika school, the concept of samsara is related to the atman, with the atman being a qualified aspect of samsara (cite: edge_015253). Samsara is also caused by karma, which is a key factor in the cycle of birth and death (cite: edge_021862). The atman is distinct from samsara, yet is still a part of it, undergoing pleasure and pain due to transmigration (cite: edge_015253). Additionally, karma is seen as a cause of samsara, leading to the cycle of birth and death (cite: edge_021850).
