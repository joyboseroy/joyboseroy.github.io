Atman

This idea is also echoed in other texts, which suggest that a realized one no longer exists after death (cite: edge_009047) and that one should give up what's not theirs, implying a separation from atman (cite: edge_008723). Samsara
In Buddhist philosophy, samsara is a concept that is distinct from the idea of atman, or self (cite: edge_008894). According to Buddhist teachings, karma is a cause of samsara, meaning that an individual's actions can lead to their continued existence in this cycle (cite: edge_010040). The concept of atman is often described as being neither still existing nor no longer existing after death, highlighting its distinction from samsara (cite: edge_008906). Additionally, the actions of beings can also contribute to samsara, with some beings going to a bad place as a result of their actions (cite: edge_010181).

Jiva

Dukkha
In Buddhist philosophy, dukkha is a concept that refers to suffering, which is seen as a fundamental aspect of life (cite: edge_009731). Anatta
In Buddhist philosophy, the concept of anatta refers to the idea that there is no permanent self or soul. According to Buddhist teachings, the self, or atman, is distinct from anatta, meaning that the self is not permanent and does not exist in the same way as anatta (cite: edge_008850). In fact, the idea of a permanent self is seen as an illusion, and the self is said to be freed from reckoning in terms of consciousness (cite: edge_008946). The Buddhist concept of anatta suggests that consciousness is not-self, and that the thought "I am" occurs because of grasping form (cite: edge_008423). Overall, the Buddhist concept of anatta emphasizes the impermanence and lack of a permanent self (cite: edge_009103).
