Atman

Five schools of Indian philosophy, compared

What each school says

Advaita Vedanta
In the Advaita school of philosophy, the concept of atman is distinct from dharma, as it "has no other duty" and "shakes off both virtue and vice" (cite: edge_018650, cite: edge_018409). At the same time, atman is also considered a qualified aspect of dharma, related to "the values consisting in long life, wealth, and holiness" and "the purposefulness of the scriptures" (cite: edge_019087, cite: edge_017890). Additionally, dharma is seen as a qualified aspect of atman, "for the sake of meditation" and "Of him faith is verily the head" (cite: edge_020055, cite: edge_019772). The relationship between atman and dharma is complex, with atman being both distinct from and connected to dharma (cite: edge_018900). [Note: this summary may contain an unverified citation.]
Dvaita Vedanta
In the school of Dvaita, the atman is seen as the cause of karma, as stated in various sources, including "the body which is produced by the Kar- ma" (cite: edge_007736) and "he does of necessity works each of which would be the cause of at least ten births" (cite: edge_007447). The atman is also considered a qualified aspect of dharma, with evidence such as "Indeed He only causes the soul to do the righteous deed" (cite: edge_007630) and "merit accruing to the priests also" (cite: edge_007869). Additionally, the atman is linked to karma through phrases like "activity and release result" (cite: edge_007351) and "passing by force of Karma" (cite: edge_007201). The atman's relationship with karma and dharma is a central theme in Dvaita philosophy (cite: edge_007339).
Vishishtadvaita
In the Vishishtadvaita school of philosophy, the atman is considered a qualified aspect of dharma, as stated in the Bhagavad Gita (cite: edge_001136). The atman is also described as the supporter of the elements (cite: edge_001829) and is associated with freedom from evil and sin (cite: edge_006715). Additionally, the atman is said to be the cause of karma, with the souls using their own creative will-power to create their own worlds (cite: edge_006737). The atman is also characterized as possessing attributes such as eternal happiness and freedom from all evil (cite: edge_002023).
Achintya Bhedabheda
In the achintya_bhedabheda school of philosophy, the atman is seen as the cause of karma, as stated in the Bhagavad Gita, "the living entity is hurt by fatal weapons" (cite: edge_002768). The atman is also considered distinct from karma, with the Gita saying, "free from the repetition of birth and death" (cite: edge_004057). Additionally, the atman is viewed as a qualified aspect of prakriti, or nature, with the Gita noting that one is "compelled by your own nature" (cite: edge_004497). The atman's relationship with karma is complex, with the Gita suggesting that it can both cause and obstruct karma (cite: edge_002792, edge_004036). Dvaitadvaita
In the school of dvaitadvaita, the concept of atman is considered to be identical to brahman (cite: edge_001077). This idea is repeated throughout the school's teachings, emphasizing the oneness of atman and brahman (cite: edge_001074). The dvaitadvaita school suggests that one should "realize Me as one [identical] with what governs the beings" to understand this relationship (cite: edge_001076). This understanding of atman as identical to brahman is a central idea in dvaitadvaita philosophy (cite: edge_001075).
Buddhism (Pali Canon)
In Buddhist philosophy, the concept of atman is distinct from death, as a realized one may or may not exist after death (cite: edge_009005). The atman is also considered distinct from delusion, as those who understand themselves do not have greed, hate, and delusion in them (cite: edge_008801). Additionally, the atman is seen as distinct from dharma, with evidence suggesting that one should give up what's not theirs and be dispassionate towards it (cite: edge_008723). The relationship between atman and knowledge is also explored, with some evidence suggesting that atman is distinct from knowledge (cite: edge_009204).
Jainism (general)
In the Jain common school of philosophy, the concept of atman is related to dharma, which is seen as a qualified aspect of atman (cite: edge_012375). Dharma is also considered a cause of atman (cite: edge_011726). The relationship between dharma and atman is further emphasized by the idea that dharma is a qualified aspect of atman, as expressed in various texts (cite: edge_021581). Overall, dharma is closely tied to the concept of atman, and is seen as both a cause and a qualified aspect of it (cite: edge_028056).

Samkhya
In the school of Samkhya, the concept of atman is distinct from the soul (cite: edge_020695). According to various texts, dharma is considered a qualified aspect of atman (cite: edge_021581, edge_013849, edge_025891, edge_028124, edge_026325, edge_026360, edge_019607, edge_025695, edge_011729, edge_011978). Dharma is also seen as a cause of atman in some texts (cite: edge_011979, edge_028056, edge_011726). These ideas suggest that dharma plays a significant role in understanding the concept of atman (cite: edge_011978). [Note: this summary may contain an unverified citation.]
Yoga
In the school of yoga, the concept of atman is closely related to dharma. Dharma is considered a qualified aspect of atman, meaning it is a part of or related to the atman (cite: edge_012375). Additionally, dharma is seen as a cause of atman, suggesting that it has an influence on the development or realization of the atman (cite: edge_011726). The relationship between dharma and atman is also described as dharma being an aspect or attribute of the Self, which is another term for atman (cite: edge_011978). Overall, the connection between dharma and atman is seen as important for spiritual growth and self-realization (cite: edge_028056).
Vaisheshika
According to the Vaisheshika school of philosophy, dharma is considered a qualified aspect of atman (cite: edge_012375). Dharma is also seen as a cause of atman (cite: edge_011726). The relationship between dharma and atman is further emphasized, with dharma being a qualified aspect of atman (cite: edge_021581). Additionally, dharma is viewed as a means to understand the eternal nature of atman (cite: edge_013849).
