What each school says

Advaita Vedanta
In the school of Advaita, dharma is often seen as a qualified aspect of karma (cite: edge_018544). This means that virtuous deeds and duties are considered part of one's karma, and can be helpful in gaining knowledge (cite: edge_018604). However, it's also noted that dharma is distinct from karma in some cases, such as in the context of certain sacrifices (cite: edge_018126). Overall, the relationship between dharma and karma in Advaita is complex, with dharma being seen as a means to achieve knowledge and ward off evil consequences, while also being influenced by scriptural teachings (cite: edge_019482).
Dvaita Vedanta
In the school of Dvaita, the concept of karma is closely related to the atman, or individual self, which is considered the cause of karma (cite: edge_008060). The atman's actions and virtues can lead to certain results, such as the body being produced by karma (cite: edge_007736). Dharma, or righteous living, is also seen as a qualified aspect of karma, with certain paths and duties being permitted (cite: edge_000250). Additionally, the atman's actions can lead to multiple births, highlighting the complex relationship between the atman, dharma, and karma (cite: edge_007447).
Vishishtadvaita
In the school of Vishishtadvaita, karma is considered a qualified aspect of dharma, meaning that it is connected to one's duties and responsibilities (cite: edge_006524). Karma is also seen as something that can be influenced by the atman, or the individual self, with the atman being the cause of karma (cite: edge_001850). Additionally, karma can have an impact on the atman, with karma being a cause of the atman's experiences (cite: edge_006212). The relationship between karma and dharma is complex, with karma being a part of dharma and dharma being a cause of karma (cite: edge_006522).

Karma is also considered a qualified aspect of dharma, or duty, with certain actions being considered appropriate or inappropriate (cite: edge_004432). The atman is distinct from karma and is not entangled by the activities performed due to contact with material bodies (cite: edge_004057). Additionally, dharma is seen as a qualified aspect of karma, with prescribed duties being performed with a certain mentality (cite: edge_004368).
